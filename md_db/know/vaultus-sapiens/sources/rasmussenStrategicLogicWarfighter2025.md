## Annotations
### Page 2- Highlight
> <mark class="yellow">In the absence of timely and well-defined strategic guidance,  military professionals are often left to infer priorities and direction. This lack of clarity poses a  significant challenge for uniformed military leaders tasked with developing military plans and  conducting future force planning. Moreover, even when formal guidance is issued, it often shifts  rapidly in response to a dynamic global environment. These conditions underscore the need for a  logical framework that brings clarity, imposes structure, and enables military leaders to align  operational actions with evolving policy intent.</mark>
### Page 3- Highlight
> <mark class="yellow">This paper proceeds in five parts. First it outlines the strategic logic that guides military  professionals, offering a visual model of the necessary coherence between national objectives  and operational actions. Second, it explores the foundational relationship between politics,  policy, and strategy, and how these elements shape military planning. Third, it explores the  distinction between strategy and grand strategy, highlighting how the traditional ends, ways, and  means framework equips planners to translate national objectives into flexible and effective  strategic plans. Fourth, in times of policy ambiguity or evolution, it highlights alternative signals  and tools that practitioners can use to infer strategic priorities. Fifth and finally, it examines how  various military plans operationalize national strategy, ensuring that military efforts remain  aligned with national policy priorities. Sustaining this alignment requires continuous education,  and the intellectual agility needed to navigate complexity and lead effectively.</mark>
### Page 5
> [!note] Comment
> It is antithetical to an "apolitical" military to expect its top leaders to intuit the strategies expected of them based solely on the political landscape and in the absence of written or flimsy policy.
### Page 10- Highlight
> <mark class="yellow">In the absence of a published NSS, military practitioners must turn to alternative sources  to infer the administration’s strategic priorities. This process requires not only attentiveness to  official communications but also a disciplined approach to interpreting a wide array of signals  that reflect strategic intent.</mark>

### Page 14- Highlight
> <mark class="yellow">The delayed 2025 NSS highlights a broad truth: military professionals must navigate  somewhat ambiguous policy shaped by shifting priorities, competing interests, and global  uncertainty. Yet even when published, strategic documents like the NSS are not static, they are  influenced by real-time decisions from civilian leaders and the changing international  environment. As a result, the responsibility of Combatant Commands (CCMDs) to translate  policy into theater-level strategy is inherently dynamic, demanding continuous interpretation and  adaptation.</mark>
### Page 14
> [!note] Comment
> War and Politics are inherently on a continuum, they cannot (and should not) be separated. Ultimately, it is the job of statesmen to draw a line in the sand where civilian influence trumps military concerns. The greater leeway we give to military leaders to interpret political priorities, the fuzzier that line gets.
