## Annotations
### Page 7- Highlight
> <mark class="yellow">Viewed in isolation, each of these concerns could plausibly be identified as a national security priority. The problem is that by ceaselessly accumulating such paramount concerns, the executive branch has made the concept increasingly meaningless.</mark>
### Page 9- Highlight
> <mark class="yellow">Entrenched political dynamics in Washington also push more and more issues onto the national security platter. The Pentagon is much better funded than the State Department; it is easier to sell security than diplomacy to Congress and the American people. In a world of constrained budgets, policy entrepreneurs are willing to frame their pet issues as national security concerns to unlock resources from the Department of Defense.</mark>
### Page 14- Highlight
> <mark class="yellow">Calibrating national security priorities has always been a challenge for U.S. officials. In January -.&gt;2, Secretary of State Dean Acheson famously delivered a National Press Club speech in which he specified which parts of the globe were within the U.S. “defense perimeter.” He did not include the Korean Peninsula. Nonetheless, when North Korea invaded South Korea less than six months later, the Truman administration deployed I22,222 troops. Korea was not a U.S. national security priority—until it was.</mark>
